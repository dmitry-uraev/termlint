Introduction

The Python Standard Library contributes significantly to Python's success.
The language comes with "batteries included", so it is easy for people to
become productive with just the standard library alone. It is therefore
important that the usefulness of the standard library be maintained.

Because of the visibility and importance of the standard library, it must be
maintained thoughtfully. Any code added to it must be maintained by Python's
development team, which creates a long-term cost for each addition. There is
also added cognitive load for users who need to understand what belongs in
the standard library.

New functionality is commonly added in the form of new modules. This document
describes the procedure for adding new modules. PEP 4 covers deprecation and
removal of old and unused modules from the standard library.

Acceptance Procedure

For top-level modules or packages, a PEP is required. The procedure for
writing a PEP is covered in PEP 1.

For submodules of an existing package in the standard library, additions are
at the discretion of the general Python development team and its members.

General guidance about which modules are typically accepted into the standard
library, and the overall process, is covered in the Python developer guide:
https://devguide.python.org/stdlibchanges/

Maintenance Procedure

Anything accepted into the standard library is expected to be primarily
maintained there, within Python's development infrastructure.

Some members of the development team may choose to maintain a backport of a
module outside of the standard library. In that case, they are responsible for
keeping external code in sync as appropriate.
